# Brand Identity Sheet: Talking to Machines

## Brand Essence

**One line:** An AI literacy curriculum disguised as a beautiful magazine.

**Brand personality:** The cool older cousin who happens to work in tech — smart, warm, zero condescension. Talks *with* teens, not *at* them. Makes complex ideas feel approachable without dumbing them down.

**Audience:** Curious teens (14–18) who are already using AI but haven't thought deeply about *how* to use it well. Digital natives who have high design standards and can smell corporate edu-content instantly.

---

## Voice & Tone

| Attribute | Is | Is Not |
|---|---|---|
| Smart | Precise, informed, current | Academic, jargon-heavy, textbook |
| Warm | Conversational, encouraging, "you" | Sycophantic, patronizing, fake-excited |
| Playful | Witty, surprising, self-aware | Random, meme-dependent, trying too hard |
| Editorial | Opinionated, curated, confident | Neutral, exhaustive, encyclopedic |
| Visual-first | Shows before tells, lets design carry weight | Wall-of-text, clip-art-decorated |

## Color System

### Primary palette — five "signal colors," each tied to thematic threads

| Color | Hex | Role | Emotional register |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coral Red | `#E94560` | Primary accent. Energy, action, urgency. | "Pay attention — this matters" |
| Electric Purple | `#7B61FF` | Creativity, AI "magic," the unknown | "This is where it gets interesting" |
| Teal | `#16C79A` | Growth, success, human capability | "You've got this" |
| Amber | `#F5A623` | Caution, insight, discovery | "Think about this carefully" |
| Sky Blue | `#0EA5E9` | Breadth, landscape, exploration | "Look how big this world is" |

### Neutral foundations

| Color | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Deep | `#1A1A2E` | Near-black. Body text, anchoring. |
| Subtle | `#6B7280` | Captions, metadata, secondary text |
| Cream | `#F8F6F3` | Warm background. Never cold white. |
| Lavender | `#EEF2FF` | Interactive element backgrounds |

### Usage rules

- Background is always warm (cream), never stark white or dark mode
- Signal colors appear in small, intentional doses — accent bars, highlights, interactive borders — never as large fills
- When multiple signal colors appear together (like in the network graph), they should feel like a constellation, not a rainbow
- Text is always Deep or Subtle — never colored body text except for inline code or concept labels

## Typography

| Role | Family | Weight | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Display / Headings | Playfair Display | 700–800 | Elegant, editorial authority. The "magazine cover" voice. |
| Body | Source Serif 4 | 400 (regular), 600 (emphasis) | Highly readable serif. Warm and literary without being stuffy. |
| Code / Technical | JetBrains Mono | 400, 600 | Clean monospace. Signals "this is precise / technical." |
| Handwritten accents | Caveat | 500–600 | Informal, human. Used *very* sparingly — annotations, asides, the occasional label. Should feel like someone scribbled in the margin. |

### Typography rules

- Headings are always Playfair. Body is always Source Serif. Mixing these roles breaks the hierarchy.
- Caveat is a seasoning, not a main ingredient. Max one instance per screen/section.
- Line height for body text: 1.7. Generous. This is a reading experience.
- Max content width: 680px. Tighter than most sites — this forces focus and feels intentional.

## Visual Motif: The Network Graph

The project's core visual metaphor is a **network of connected nodes** — colorful dots linked by gradient lines, dense at the edges, clear at the center where text lives.

### What it represents

- Connections between ideas, tools, people, and machines
- The neural/graph nature of AI itself
- A constellation — something to navigate by, not a rigid structure
- Complexity that resolves into clarity (dense edges → clear center)

### How it's used

- Share cards and OG images
- Potential logo mark element
- Section dividers and decorative backgrounds
- Never as a literal diagram — always abstract, atmospheric

## Logo Design Brief

**The name:** "Talking to Machines" — six syllables, two conceptual halves. "Talking to" is human, casual, everyday. "Machines" is precise, technical, slightly alien. The logo should hold that tension.

### Requirements

1. **Wordmark-primary.** The name is strong and distinctive enough to carry itself. A custom wordmark in Playfair Display (or a bespoke serif in that spirit) should be the primary logo.

2. **"Machines" gets the accent.** The word "Machines" should be visually differentiated — coral red (`#E94560`), a different weight, or a subtle stylistic shift. This mirrors the existing share card treatment and creates a natural two-part rhythm: *[Talking to]* `[Machines]`.

3. **Optional logomark.** A small symbol that works at favicon/app-icon scale. Directions to explore:
   - **Abstract network node:** A single node with 3–5 radiating connection lines, using 2–3 signal colors. Simple enough for 32×32.
   - **Speech-to-circuit:** A speech bubble whose tail or outline transitions into a circuit/node pattern — the literal intersection of "talking" and "machines."
   - **Cursor-meets-pen:** A text cursor (`|`) that subtly morphs into or sits beside a human element — a slight serif flourish, an ink-like quality.
   - **Constellation dot cluster:** 3–5 dots in signal colors arranged in a small, recognizable pattern (like a mini version of the network graph). No lines needed at small scale — just the dots.

4. **What to avoid:**
   - Robot faces, chat bubbles with "..." , or any literal AI/robot imagery. This project is about *humans* getting better at working with AI, not about the AI itself.
   - Gradients as a crutch. The multi-color system is already rich — the logo should work in single-color (Deep on Cream, or reversed).
   - Overly techy/startup aesthetics. This should feel editorial and literary, closer to a magazine masthead than a SaaS logo.
   - Anything that reads as "for kids." The audience is teens with taste. Aim for the sophistication of a NYT interactive or Pudding.cool piece.

5. **Must work in these contexts:**
   - Share card hero (108px, as seen in the existing LinkedIn card)
   - Site header (roughly 28–36px height)
   - Favicon (16×16, 32×32) — logomark only
   - Single-color on dark or light backgrounds
   - Alongside the tagline: *"The future belongs to those who ask the right questions."*

## Tagline

**Primary:** *The future belongs to those who ask the right questions.*

**Short/alternate:** *Learn to speak AI.* (more direct, useful for social/ads)

---

## Brand "Don'ts"

- Don't use dark mode. The warmth of cream is core to the identity.
- Don't use stock illustrations of robots or brains.
- Don't center-align body text. Left-aligned, always. (Center is reserved for hero/display moments.)
- Don't use more than 2 signal colors in a single UI element.
- Don't describe the project as a "course" or "class" in brand materials — it's a *curriculum*, *experience*, or *guide*. "Course" sounds like homework.
